## Why "Keplore"

The name is a tribute to Johannes Kepler (1571–1630), who derived the three laws of
planetary motion — not through pure reasoning, but through the combination of precise
observational data and mathematical inference.

Kepler's data came from his mentor Tycho Brahe, a Danish astronomer who spent decades
building sophisticated instruments and collecting the most precise planetary observations
of his era. Tycho constructed the Uraniborg observatory and purpose-built measurement
devices to capture data that no one else had. Without that data, Kepler's laws could
not have been derived.

Before Kepler, astronomy was dominated by pure geometric reasoning — Ptolemy's epicycles,
Copernicus's circles. The models were logically coherent but wrong, because they were
not grounded in precise measurement. Kepler's laws work because they are derived from
real data, constrained by real observations, and therefore capable of making reliable
predictions about where a planet will be.

Keplore takes the same approach to industrial AI. Generic AI reasoning alone —
pattern-matching over public data — produces plausible-sounding output that cannot be
trusted in industrial production. Keplore's skills are the "Tycho Brahe data": precise,
domain-specific, expert-verified knowledge that grounds the AI agent's reasoning and
execution. The result is not approximation. It is reliable work.

## Problem Statement

**The fundamental shift underway:** Industrial work is transitioning from "AI assistant + human does the work" to "AI does the work + human assists." In the old model, a human engineer uses AI as a tool — AI suggests, human executes. In the new model, AI agents execute the work — building systems, deploying models, integrating with hardware — and the human oversees, approves, and guides.

This is not a knowledge retrieval problem. It is an expertise execution problem.

Industrial companies already have tools for the light tasks: error analysis, root cause diagnosis, workflow automation, data dashboards, document Q&A. These are useful support functions, but they are not the core work. The core work is heavy: building an AI vision system that reliably detects defects on a production line, deploying and integrating it with existing hardware, calibrating it against real performance signals, and keeping it working as conditions change. This is the work that currently requires skilled engineers, weeks of iteration, and domain expertise that is nearly impossible to transfer. This is the bottleneck — and it is what Keplore is solving.

Industrial companies (manufacturing, robotics, EV R&D) face two compounding problems that block AI from doing this heavy work:

**Problem 1: Generic AI can't access industrial knowledge.**
Generic AI tools (Cursor, Openhands, ChatGPT) cannot solve industrial-domain problems
because the required knowledge — safety regulations, proprietary workflows, integration
protocols, domain-specific scripts, private operational data — does not exist in any
public training corpus. Generic AI produces plausible-sounding output but cannot be
trusted in production.

**Problem 2: Industrial knowledge can't be executed by AI agents.**
Industrial expertise exists in two forms, and neither works with today's AI:

- **Undocumented knowledge** lives only in individual engineers' heads — the intuition
  built over years on a specific machine, line, or system. It is not shareable, not
  transferable, and walks out the door when the expert leaves.

- **Documented knowledge** — SOPs, runbooks, calibration procedures, integration scripts —
  exists on paper or in wikis, but existing AI systems can only use it at the information
  retrieval level: RAG pipelines, document Q&A, text search. The knowledge gets referenced
  and cited. It never gets executed. An agent can tell you what the threshold tuning
  procedure says. It cannot actually tune the threshold, run the validation, integrate
  the result with the PLC, and confirm the system is operational.

The undocumented case is worse. But even documented knowledge fails at the action level.

Previous approaches to infusing expertise into AI built a bigger dictionary or a bigger
book. Keplore makes agents that can actually work — using expertise knowledge to build
systems, execute solutions, operate deployments, integrate with industrial infrastructure,
and validate results.

Keplore's solution: a skills-oriented co-worker platform where industrial knowledge is
packaged as executable skills — code, data, and domain instructions that agents invoke
directly. The agent doesn't look up the procedure. It runs it. Both AI agents and new
human teammates can apply expertise consistently, without months of coaching.

Current state: Keplore's engineers manually operate generic AI tools to build and
maintain industrial AI solutions for customers. The engineers are the product today at the initial stage.

## Why Agent Skills — Not Training Data

The obvious alternative to Keplore's approach is: collect industrial data, fine-tune
a model, and give it domain expertise through training. This is what the scaling law
thesis implies — more data, better model. It is the path that does not work in
industrial domains, for four structural reasons:

**1. Models learn repeat patterns — industrial tasks require principles.**
This is the most fundamental limitation. Large models, whether trained on text or
images, abstract repeat patterns from data. Pattern learning works well for language
generation and image synthesis, where approximate outputs are acceptable. It does not
work for industrial tasks, where correctness depends on precision, mathematical rules,
physical laws, and operational logic.

A well-known symptom: ask a large model whether 1.13 or 1.8 is larger. A pattern-
trained model often answers 1.13, because it has seen more digits and the pattern
"more digits = larger number" dominates. The model has learned the surface pattern,
not the underlying numerical principle. In industrial settings, this failure mode is
everywhere — and the consequences are not a wrong answer in a chat window, they are
a miscalibrated machine, a failed deployment, or a safety incident.

A person who only observes patterns will eventually draw wrong conclusions. A person
who learns physics, chemistry, and domain principles can reason correctly even in
situations they have never seen before. More training data gives models more patterns.
It does not give them principles. Industrial expertise is principled knowledge, not
pattern knowledge — and scaling cannot close that gap.

**2. Not enough data exists.** Industrial knowledge is not publicly available,
well-organized, or digitized at scale. A specific machine's calibration parameters,
a PLC integration's quirks, a factory's safety protocol — none of this appears in
any training corpus, and collecting it is years of work per customer.

**3. The data types are wrong.** Industrial expertise lives in video demos, physical
procedures, hardware-specific configuration, proprietary business logic, and
operational intuition. Text-based training data cannot capture the form in which
this knowledge actually exists.

**4. Training is too expensive and too slow per customer.** Fine-tuning a model on
proprietary data for each customer — then managing drift, retraining on updates,
and maintaining per-customer model versions — is an operational nightmare. The cost
and latency make it non-viable for the customers Keplore serves.

The scaling law path is structurally blocked for industrial AI.

**Agent skills are a different path — and a new historical moment.** Instead of
injecting expertise through training, skills package expert knowledge as structured,
organized, executable artifacts: code that runs, calibration data that gets used,
domain instructions that guide the agent at inference time. The agent doesn't need
to have been trained on the domain. It invokes the skill, and the skill carries the
expertise.

Skills are fundamentally data — but a new form of data. Not training data that shapes
model weights. Executable data that extends model capability without retraining, at
the moment the agent needs it, scoped to the task at hand.

**Skills are also the answer to reliability — which is the real requirement for task
execution.** Snowflake and Aitomatic may also claim to solve the "enterprise knowledge"
problem. The distinction is not the knowledge claim. It is what the agent does with
that knowledge. Their systems answer questions, generate reports, surface insights.
The output is text. Text can be approximately right.

Industrial task execution cannot be approximately right. When an agent is configuring
a vision system, running a calibration script, or integrating with a production PLC,
the output is a running system — or a failed deployment. Probabilistic pattern-matching
produces unpredictable results across executions. Skills produce deterministic behavior:
the same code runs, the same calibration data is applied, the same validated procedure
is followed. Every time.

This is why skills are the right primitive for industrial execution — not because they
store more knowledge, but because they make AI behavior reliable enough to trust in
production environments where the cost of being wrong is not a bad sentence but a
broken machine.

**This makes Keplore a data company.** The product is a platform for creating,
organizing, validating, and deploying structured expert knowledge — in a form that
AI agents can execute, not just reference. The skills library is the asset. Every
delivery project adds to it. Every new customer starts further ahead because of it.

## Status Quo

Customers solve this with: generic AI tools (Cursor, Openhands) + Keplore's engineers
+ the customer's own domain experts.

Three knowledge silos exist simultaneously:
1. **Keplore engineers** — know how to apply AI to industrial problems, but this
   expertise doesn't scale beyond headcount
2. **Customer domain engineers** — know the specific workflows, safety constraints,
   and domain logic for their area, but this knowledge is trapped in their head or private documents and
   doesn't automatically transfer to new colleagues or new problem types
3. **Customer managers** — hold institutional knowledge about why processes work the
   way they do, accumulated over years, often never written down

The result: every new project, new teammate, and new use case requires re-educating
people from scratch. Training a new engineer to competence takes months. Different
engineers produce inconsistent results because their mental models diverge. When a
senior expert leaves, the company loses capability it cannot easily rebuild.

Generic AI makes this worse in one way: it creates the illusion of a shortcut. But
because industrial knowledge isn't in the public corpus, generic AI confidently produces
wrong answers — and catching those errors requires the same expert who was needed before.

## Core Business Model: Vertical AI Skills Library

**The one thing that matters most:** a large, deep library of private agent skills
covering industrial verticals. Everything else — the platform, the delivery work, the
customer relationships — is either a means to build this library or a way to sell
access to it.

**The Scale AI analogy.** Scale AI built a business by hiring large numbers of workers
to label training data, then selling that labeled data to AI companies who needed it
to train models. The core insight: the bottleneck was not the AI, it was the data.
Scale AI solved the data supply problem at scale through labor.

Keplore is the same model, one layer up. The bottleneck for industrial AI agents is
not the AI model — Claude is capable. The bottleneck is executable skills: the
structured, expert-verified, domain-specific packages that let an agent actually do
industrial work correctly. This is hard to build. It requires people with industrial
domain knowledge to go into factories, capture workflows, write and validate code, and
package expertise as skills. That is labor-intensive. It does not happen automatically.

**The technical workforce model.** Build the skills library by deploying technical
engineers — people who can write code, organize domain knowledge into structured files,
and validate execution against real equipment — into factories and industrial sites
across China, Vietnam, and Southeast Asia. This is not a click-labor model. It requires
coding ability, documentation discipline, and domain learning. The people doing this
work are a core company asset, not interchangeable contractors.

They compound. An engineer who has built 20 skills across 3 factory deployments builds
the next skill faster and better than someone starting from scratch. Domain familiarity,
pattern recognition across industrial systems, and fluency with the SKILL.md format
all accumulate. The workforce gets more productive over time.

They can also be accelerated. Keplore's own platform — skills + AI agents — can be
applied internally to speed up the skill-building process itself. Boilerplate generation,
code scaffolding, documentation drafting, validation script templates: these are all
tasks the agent can assist with. The more mature the skills library, the faster new
skills get built. The process is itself skill-able.

**Category: Vertical AI.** Not a horizontal AI platform. Not a general-purpose agent
framework. A vertical-specific skills library for industrial domains — deep coverage
in manufacturing, robotics, vision systems, EV R&D — that any industrial AI agent can
invoke. The more skills in the library, the more verticals covered, the more
autonomous the agent becomes in those domains.

**Revenue model: two phases.**

*Phase 1 (now — library building):* Deliver solutions to enterprise customers. Charge
for the work, but margin is not the goal. Keplore can afford to price delivery below
market — or even at cost — because the long-term asset being built is the skills
library, not the delivery revenue. Every engagement produces validated skills. The
delivery work is the R&D budget for the library. Three current customers (China
manufacturer, Geely, Idaho SI) are phase 1.

This is a durable competitive advantage during phase 1: competitors who need to make
money on delivery cannot undercut Keplore if Keplore doesn't need to make money on
delivery. Keplore can take on engagements others won't, cover verticals others can't
afford to enter, and move faster because skills from previous projects carry forward.

*Phase 2 (library monetization):* Once the library has depth and breadth, sell access —
subscription, per-skill licensing, or per-domain packages. At that point the marginal
cost of serving an additional customer is near zero. The customers who paid for phase 1
delivery work have already funded the asset. Phase 2 is when the business becomes a
software business.

Light-task companies (Aitomatic, Snowflake) that want to add execution capability can
buy skills from Keplore rather than building industrial domain expertise themselves.
The library becomes infrastructure.

**The key difference from Scale AI's training data.** Scale AI's output is consumed
once — data trains a model, then the data's job is done. Keplore's skills are invoked
repeatedly: every time an agent runs a calibration, deploys a model, or integrates
with a PLC, it draws on the same skill packages. The library appreciates in value with
every invocation and every validation. Skills that have been run against real production
equipment are more valuable than skills that haven't. Usage is verification.

## Skill Architecture — Claude Agent SDK Paradigm

Skills follow the official Claude Agent SDK "skills" pattern. A skill is a filesystem
artifact: a `SKILL.md` file with YAML frontmatter + optional bundled scripts. Claude
autonomously discovers and invokes skills based on description matching — no custom
embedding router required.

**Skill structure:**

A skill is a complete, self-contained package: the `SKILL.md` is the interface
(what it does, when to invoke it, how to use it), but the actual capability lives
in source code and static resources bundled alongside it.

```
keplore-skills/
  {tenant-id}/
    {role}/
      {skill-name}/
        SKILL.md          ← interface: frontmatter + instructions for Claude
        scripts/
          tune.py         ← executable source code (ML, vision, integration logic)
          evaluate.py
          deploy.sh
        data/
          thresholds.csv  ← calibration tables, lookup data
          safety_limits/  ← regulatory reference files
          reference_models/  ← pre-trained model weights or configs
        tests/
          validate.py     ← validation scripts Claude can run to verify output
```

The `SKILL.md` describes the package. The `scripts/` and `data/` are what make it
execute. A skill for "magnetic defect threshold tuning" isn't just knowledge — it
includes the actual Python code that runs the threshold sweep, the calibration data
specific to this customer's machines, and validation scripts that confirm the result
is within safety margins before deployment.

**SKILL.md format:**
```markdown
---
name: magnetic-defect-threshold-tuning
description: >
  Tunes sensitivity thresholds for magnetic defect detection vision models.
  Use when adjusting detection sensitivity, reducing false positives, calibrating
  ROC parameters, or adapting an existing vision model to a new product variant.
  Applies this customer's proprietary threshold calibration methodology and
  safety margin requirements. Do NOT use for initial model training — only
  for threshold adjustment on trained models.
owner: keplore_engineer   # or customer_engineer
shared: false             # true = readable by all tenants (Keplore-controlled)
version: 1
---

# Magnetic Defect Threshold Tuning

## When to use this skill
[domain-specific guidance on task scoping]

## Domain knowledge
[industrial knowledge: calibration methodology, safety constraints, regulatory refs]

## Validation requirements
[what must be verified before deploying threshold changes]

## Bundled tools
- `scripts/tune_threshold.py` — runs threshold sweep and outputs ROC curve data
```

**How skill selection works:**
Claude reads all SKILL.md frontmatter metadata at session start (low token cost: ~100
tokens per skill). When a task arrives, Claude autonomously decides which skills are
relevant based on description matching — no custom router or embedding pipeline needed.
This is the behavior the model is trained for. If no skill matches, Claude falls back
to general capability + human escalation flag.

**Expert role = a directory of skills** sharing a domain:
```
{tenant}/vision-model-engineer/     ← role directory
  magnetic-defect-threshold-tuning/ ← skill
  battery-cell-inspection-setup/    ← skill
  camera-calibration/               ← skill
{tenant}/robot-integration-engineer/
  halcon-scripting/
  plc-integration/
```

**Three-tier loading (Claude Agent SDK native):**
- Metadata (always loaded): `name` + `description` from frontmatter — ~100 tokens/skill
- Instructions (loaded when triggered): full SKILL.md body — <5k tokens/skill
- Scripts (executed when invoked): run without loading content into context

**Skill ownership and tenancy:**
- `owner: keplore_engineer` — only Keplore team may edit
- `owner: customer_engineer` — tenant's designated engineers may edit
- `shared: true` — Keplore-controlled, read-only across tenants (generic industrial patterns)
- Each tenant's skill directory is isolated; skills don't cross tenant boundaries unless shared

**Autoresearch is deferred to v2.** v0-v1 skills are human-authored and human-updated.
Automated skill improvement requires sufficient usage data and the risk of automated
degradation in safety-critical industrial systems is high.

## Multi-Tenancy and Auth Model (v0)

Skills and data are namespaced by `tenant_id` at the storage layer. Keplore's internal
skills are one tenant. Each customer deployment (Geely, China manufacturer) is a separate
tenant. Cross-tenant skill sharing: `shared: true` flag in skill YAML, Keplore-controlled,
shared skills are read-only for all tenants.

Auth: API key per tenant. Keplore engineers hold a super-admin key with read access
across all tenants. Customers receive a scoped key for their tenant only. SSO deferred to v2.

Skill version history is append-only. Previous versions are retained in git. Rollback
in v0 is manual (git revert the SKILL.md to a prior version).

Human review gate: for Segment A customers, all AI-generated outputs are reviewed by a
Keplore engineer before delivery. For Segment B (Geely), customers designate a reviewer
role; the platform surfaces the skill's `validation_criteria` as a checklist.
This is an explicit design choice for v0 — not a fallback.

## Competitive Differentiation

### The category distinction: light tasks vs. heavy tasks

Most "enterprise AI" companies — Aitomatic, Snowflake, and others — solve the **light
tasks**: analyzing errors, diagnosing root causes, reviewing documents, generating
reports, building workflows. These are real problems. They are not the core work of
an industrial company.

The core work is heavy: building AI systems, deploying models to production hardware,
integrating with existing industrial systems, calibrating against real performance
signals, constructing and validating complete solutions. This is what industrial
engineers spend most of their time on. This is where the highest costs, the longest
delays, and the most expensive expertise bottlenecks live.

Aitomatic's published success cases: root cause analysis, loan file review, heat
exchanger issue diagnosis — light tasks. Snowflake's: supply chain risk reports, IoT
data insights, manufacturing analytics — light tasks. These are valuable. They are
support functions, not the deliverable. The deliverable is a working system.

Keplore operates in the heavy task category: **execution and solution building.**
Inputs: a problem ("adapt last year's vision model for the new battery cell line").
Outputs: a working system — model built, calibrated, integrated with the customer's
hardware, validated, running on the machine.

That is not a workflow. That is work.

### Why information-processing tools fail at action execution

For information processing, enhanced documents are sufficient. RAG pipelines, knowledge
bases, and document Q&A work because the task is fundamentally retrieval and reasoning
over text.

For action execution, they are not enough. The agent needs to:
- Run code against real data and real hardware APIs
- Use domain-specific scripts, calibration files, and configuration parameters
- Execute in the right computing environment with the right dependencies
- Validate results against real performance signals — not against a document

A bigger wiki does not help you tune a defect detection threshold. A skill package —
`tune.py` + `thresholds.csv` + the domain instructions for when and how to run it —
does. This is why executable skills (interface + source code + static files) are the
right primitive for this category, and why document-centric approaches fall short.

### Why this matters competitively

Aitomatic cannot easily enter this category because their architecture is built for
information processing. Adding action execution requires agents, executable skill
packages, physical-world integration, and a service model that handles deployment
— not just a knowledge ingestion pipeline.

Keplore is building from the action execution category from day one. The skills
architecture is designed for this: every skill is code that runs, not a document
that gets retrieved. The customers (China manufacturer, Idaho SI, Geely) all need
systems built and running — not reports generated.

### "What if they try to do what you do?"

The methods that work for light tasks do not transfer to heavy tasks. Root cause
analysis runs on documents and sensor data. Building and deploying a vision system
runs on code, hardware APIs, calibration data, and validated execution logic. The
architecture, the service model, the required expertise, and the customer relationship
are all different. A company optimized for information processing would need to rebuild
from scratch to enter the execution category — not extend what they have.

Keplore is already executing in industrial settings. That head start compounds: every
delivery project builds the skills library, every customer adds domain coverage, every
validated execution makes the next one faster. A competitor starting now would be
building in an empty library against a team with real production experience.

Beyond competition, the relationship is complementary. Light task tools (analysis,
diagnosis, workflow) and heavy task tools (execution, solution building) serve different
parts of the same industrial company's needs. They don't compete for the same budget
line. A Geely engineer using an analysis tool to diagnose a model failure and then
using Keplore to rebuild and redeploy the model is using both — in sequence.

Long-term, Keplore's skills library becomes infrastructure others can build on. If
analysis tools want to add execution capability, they can buy skills from Keplore
rather than building domain expertise from scratch. The skills library is not just
a moat. It is a potential distribution channel.

**3-sentence answer to "why Keplore over Aitomatic":**
Aitomatic makes AI better at answering industrial questions. Keplore makes AI capable
of doing industrial work — building models, deploying systems, integrating with hardware,
validating results in the physical world. When a customer needs a root cause analysis,
Aitomatic is the right tool. When they need a working vision system on a production
line, Keplore is.

### Secondary differentiators

**Multi-domain through composable skills.** The same platform serves magnetic defect
detection (China), potato vision inspection (Idaho), and EV R&D (Geely) without
rebuilding the core for each vertical. Skills compose; domains don't conflict.

**Outcomes + tool, not just tool.** Segment A customers (no in-house AI engineers)
get outcomes delivered. Segment B customers (Geely) get a tool their engineers use
directly. Aitomatic sells tooling. Keplore can sell either — depending on what the
customer can absorb.

**Skills as compounding moat.** The accumulated skill library — private workflows,
domain scripts, calibration data — is what customers cannot replicate with generic AI
and cannot take to a competitor without rebuilding. It compounds across every customer
and every domain Keplore serves.

## Success Criteria

- **Month 1:** Keplore engineers reduce average hours-per-project by 30% vs. current
  baseline (measure against last 3 projects before tool adoption). Defect rate
  (rework after delivery) stays flat or improves.
- **Month 3:** Geely's engineers can create and activate a new skill without Keplore
  involvement. Measured: at least 1 skill created and used in production by Geely team.
- **Month 6:** China manufacturer deployment runs without engineer intervention for
  *previously resolved issue types* — known-good skills handle known-good cases.
  Novel issues still escalate to human. Measured: zero Keplore engineer hours for
  routine (non-novel) maintenance in any given 2-week period.
- **Year 1:** Skills database has >100 verified skills across 3+ domains. At least 1
  Segment B customer (Geely or new) using the tool autonomously.
