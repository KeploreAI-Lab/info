Why Generic AI Fails

Scaling law is structurally blocked
in industrial domains.

The obvious solution — train models on more industrial data — doesn't work. Four reasons, the first being the most fundamental.

Reason 01 — Most Fundamental

Models learn patterns. Industrial tasks require principles.

Large models abstract repeat patterns from data — whether text or images. Pattern learning works for language generation, where approximate output is acceptable. It does not work for industrial execution, where correctness depends on precision, mathematical rules, and physical laws.

Classic failure case

Ask a large model: is 1.13 or 1.8 larger? Pattern-trained models often answer 1.13 — because "more digits = larger number" dominates over actual numerical reasoning. In industrial settings, this failure mode causes miscalibrated machines, failed deployments, and safety incidents. More training data gives you more patterns. It does not give you principles.

Reason 02

Not enough industrial data exists.

Calibration parameters, PLC integration quirks, factory safety protocols — none of this is in any public training corpus. Collecting it is years of work per customer, per domain.

Reason 03

The data types are wrong.

Industrial expertise lives in video demos, physical procedures, hardware configuration, and proprietary business logic. Text-based training cannot capture the form in which this knowledge actually exists.

Reason 04

Per-customer training is an operational nightmare.

Fine-tuning a model on proprietary data per customer — then managing drift, retraining on updates, maintaining per-customer versions — is prohibitively expensive and slow.

Business Model

Two phases.
One compounding asset.

The delivery work and the data business are not in tension. Phase 1 funds and builds Phase 2. Every project is an investment in the library.

Phase 1 — Now

Deliver Solutions

Work directly with industrial customers to build and deploy AI solutions. Charge for delivery — but margin is not the goal. The goal is building a validated skills library that compounds.

Because Keplore isn't optimizing for delivery margin, it can take engagements competitors can't afford to, move into verticals others won't enter, and reuse skills from previous projects to move faster than anyone starting from scratch.

Current customers: China manufacturer (magnetic defect detection), Geely EV R&D, Idaho robotics SI.
Phase 2 — Library Monetization

Sell the Skills

Once the library has depth and breadth, sell access — subscription, per-skill licensing, or per-domain packages. At this point, the marginal cost of serving an additional customer is near zero.

Phase 1 customers funded the asset. Phase 2 is when the business becomes a software business. Other AI companies — including light-task platforms like Aitomatic — can buy Keplore skills to add execution capability without building domain expertise from scratch.

The skills library becomes infrastructure. Keplore becomes the data layer for industrial AI execution.
Why "Keplore"

Grounded knowledge
makes reliable predictions.

The name is a tribute to Johannes Kepler — who proved that precise observational data, not pure reasoning, is what makes scientific laws actually work.

The historical parallel

Kepler derived his three laws of planetary motion using precise observations collected by Tycho Brahe — who spent decades building sophisticated instruments and measuring planetary positions with then-unprecedented accuracy.

Before Kepler, astronomers relied on pure geometric reasoning (Ptolemy's epicycles, Copernicus's circles). Logically coherent. Wrong. Because they weren't grounded in precise measurement.

Kepler's laws work — can predict where a planet will be — because they were derived from real data and constrained by real observations. The data was decisive.
